SE modelling for causal understanding of systems

  • Meta-model aims to connect general theory with study specific model and data
  • Theory is often more general than particular proxies used (e.g. climate change includes temperature mean, extremes, precipitation changes etc.)
  • Constructs can bridge between general theory and the complexity of case studies:
    • Capture underlying processes and multiple causes
    • Interactions between variables
    • Nonlinear relationships

Motivation for SE modelling

  • So far, only observed variables were considered (i.e. path model).
  • However, many ecological concepts are:
    • abstract and difficult to quantify and measure directly
    • multifaceted and multidimensional
  • Latent and composite variables help to capture such concepts

Latent variables

Latent variables in the social sciences and psychology

Instrument development: The process of creating a new instrument [a set of specific measurements] for measuring an unobserved or latent construct, such as depression, sociability, or fourth-grade mathematics ability.

  • In the social sciences, latent variables are widely used to capture abstract concepts.
  • Human attitudes and motivations are assumed to be “deeply latent” and only discernible indirectly.
  • Moreover, the properties often of primary interest itself and used as instruments.
  • Powerful but currently underused tool in ecology.

Latent variables

  • Latent variables represented by oval node shape.
  • The latent variable itself is unobserved, its influence is captured by indicator variable(s).
  • Latents capture the common variance or overlapping information for a set of measures.
  • Direction of causality flows from latent variable to the (reflective) indicator(s).
  • Latent variables are free of random or systematic measurement errors (unlike their indicators).

Fitting latent variables

  • Latent variable can have 1 to many indicators:
    • 1 indicator:
      • assumes measured variable is perfect representation of the latent.
      • makes a conceptual distinction between the concept of interest and the observed measure.
    • 2 indicators:
      • requires assumptions to identify the model.
      • possible to remove measurement error.
    • 3 or more indicators:
      • allows to test whether or not the same process generated the indicators.

Fitting latent variables

Latents fitted in two steps:

  1. Measurement model (Confirmatory Factor Analysis)
    • Precursor to any SEM in which the latent variables appear.
    • For multi-indicator latent variables: check that the same process generated multiple indicators.
    • All indicators need to be correlated with similar loadings to justify an underlying latent variable (i.e., the cause).
  1. Structural model
    • Estimates relationships betweenmultiple latent variables and between latent and other variables.

Latent example 1: measurement error in predictor

  • Latent captures the overlapping information in the repeated measures.
  • Better slope estimate and higher R2 when regressing Y on latent.

Latent example 2: body size

  • Body size is multidimensional.
    • Multiple indicators of body size: mass, length, width
    • Identifiability:
      • Loading of first indicator set to 1 by lavaan (sets scale of latent to 1st indicator).
      • Because of this, use standardized effect for interpretation.
      • Latents with two indicators require additional assumptions (e.g. similar loadings of latent on indicators).

Latent variables - a word of caution

  • The design of latent variables requires deep thinking and justification
  • The choice of particular indicators to define a latent variable needs justification.
  • The naming fallacy: naming a latent variable in a certain way does not guarantee you capture the underling process.

Composite variables

Composite variables

  • Composites can be used flexibly and require less assumptions than latents:
    • Summarize multiple causes under a single composite (dimension reduction).
    • Allow to model interactions and nonlinear relationships.
  • Composites are shown as hexangular shapes in causal diagrams.

Example composite: joint cause landuse

  • Composites specify the collective influence of a set of variables.
  • Arrows point from the indicators to the composite (i.e., formative or causal indicators).
  • The values of the composite determined by causes (i.e., indicators), thus error variance is set to 0.
    • Technically, predictions from linear combinations of variables.

Example composite: joint cause landuse

  • Estimation can cause convergence issues, lavaan can only handle a single composite per model.
    • Solution: hand-code composites.
  • Only standardised coefficients interpretable.

Example composite: model nonlinear effects

  • Nutrient effects on biomass may be nonlinear (e.g. hump-shaped)
  • Can be modelled by adding a linear and a squared term of nutrients
  • Check validity of nonlinear effect by only constructing composite first (linear model)

Distinguishing latent and composite variables

Rules of thumb:

  • Latent:
    • indicators are redundant, e.g. 
      • multiple measurements of the same process
      • multiple proxies of the same process
  • Composite:
    • the meaning of the construct changes after dropping one of the indicators

Interactions

Interactions

  • Effect of one variable may depend on another variable.
  • E.g., nutrient effect on plant growth, may depend on whether the environment was disturbed in the past.
  • The interaction coefficient captures possible positive or negative deviation from additivity (e.g., the sum of the two effects in isolation).
  • In a regression model, interaction coefficient estimates the effect of the product of the two predictors.

Interactions

Two ways to model interactions in lavaan:

  1. Interactions between continous variables: composite variable
  2. Interactions with categorical variables: multi-group SEM

Interactions between continuous variables (composite)

  • Include interaction term in composite.
  • Preferable when only a few interactions are considered.

Interactions with categorical variable (multi-group)

Group = Disturbed

Group = Undisturbed

  • Multi-group SEM allows all coefficients to vary among groups.
  • Groups have to be categorical (e.g., treatment, age class).
  • Allows formal tests of equality among causal diagrams

Interactions with categorical variable (multi-group)

  • Test interaction of a categorical factor with many or all paths in the causal diagram.
  • Lavaan allows to test various equality constraints:
    • Equal path coefficients, intercepts, residuals among groups
  • Even more control by naming and fixing parameters
    • Coefficients with same name are estimated to be the same
    • Fixing coefficients to 0 allows graph to vary among groups.
